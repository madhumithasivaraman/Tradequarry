Bar Chart

What is Bar Chart?

A bar chart, also known as a bar graph, visually represents data using rectangular bars whose lengths are proportional to the values they depict. However, in trading and technical analysis, the concept of a bar chart takes on a distinct meaning. Commonly referred to as an OHLC (open, high, low, close) bar chart, it illustrates the price movements of a financial asset over a specific period.

The strength of bar charts lies in their simplicity and versatility. Traditional bar charts are valuable tools for traders to visualize financial information, such as tracking trading volume over time or analyzing the financial statements of various companies as part of fundamental analysis.

On the other hand, OHLC bar charts serve technical analysts similarly to candlestick charts. Mastering the fundamentals of bar charts — from their straightforward layout to their effective interpretation — is an essential skill in the realm of finance.

Key Highlights

•A bar chart visually depicts the opening, high, low, and closing prices of an asset or security over a specified period of time.

•The vertical line on a price bar represents the high and low prices for the period.

•The left and right horizontal lines on each price bar represent the opening and closing prices.

•Bar charts can be color-coded where if the close is above the open it may be colored black or green, and if the close is below the open the bar may be colored red.

•Bar and candlestick charts, while similar, have different presentation styles. Candlestick charts provide more visual depth, highlighting price patterns and market sentiment.

Bar chart vs other charts

•Line graphs: are better suited for illustrating trends over time.


•Pie charts: Often utilized to depict proportions of a whole.


•Scatter plots: Employed to unveil relationships between two numeric variables.


•Bar charts: Utilized for contrasting numerical values across separate categories.

Bar chart in Trading

Bar charts in trading and technical analysis, often referred to as 'OHLC' (open, high, low, close) bar charts, have a different objective and structure.

Each 'bar' here represents a specified period— a day, an hour, a minute, depending on the chosen time frame. A single bar contains information about the opening price, the closing price and the highest and lowest prices during that period. Colour coding, typically green or blue for bullish movements and red for bearish ones, adds an extra layer of interpretative ease.


The objective of employing these charts is to visually represent the price fluctuations of a financial asset across time intervals, facilitating the detection of trends and patterns essential for informed trading judgments.

Although both conventional and OHLC (Open, High, Low, Close) bar charts are commonly referred to by the same name, their compositions, applications, and analyses differ, underscoring the varied applications of data visualization methods in diverse domains.



Direction and Range

The direction the price has moved during the bar is indicated by the locations of the opening and closing feet. If the closing foot is above the opening foot, then the price made upward progress during the bar. If the closing foot is below the opening foot, then the price made downward progress during the bar.

The range of the bar is indicated by the locations of the top and bottom of the vertical bar. The range is calculated by subtracting the low from the high (Range of Bar = High - Low).

How to use bar charts in trading and investing

Here,is the example for bar chart in Trading



•Fundamental Analysis: Traditional bar charts can be employed to visually depict essential financial metrics derived from company balance sheets, such as revenues, net income, or earnings per share (EPS) over various years. This facilitates a transparent comparison of a company's financial well-being over time.

•Trading Volume Examination: Investors can utilize a standard bar chart to graph trading volumes of a specific security across time periods. This graphical representation aids in the identification of trends, patterns, and periods of heightened trading activity.

•Sector Performance Evaluation: A typical bar chart can illustrate the performance of diverse sectors within a specified timeframe, providing investors with insights into the areas of market strength.

•Economic Indicators Representation: Standard bar charts are effective tools for illustrating macroeconomic indicators like gross domestic product (GDP) growth, unemployment rates, or inflation rates. This enables investors to comprehend economic trends and make informed decisions.

•Technical Analysis: Leveraging OHLC (Open, High, Low, Close) bar charts, traders can discern market trends, detect price reversals, identify price patterns, analyze market volatility, compare opening and closing prices, and observe high and low price points. These insights effectively inform trading strategies and decisions.
